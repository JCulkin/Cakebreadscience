Electricity and Circuits

IGCSE Edexcel Physics
2.1–2.21 Electricity and Circuits
Key Concepts: Current is the rate of flow of charge. Voltage is energy per charge. Series and parallel circuits behave differently. Power and energy can be calculated using $P = IV$ and $E = IVt$.

Section A: Units and Safety

1. State the SI units for current, charge, voltage, resistance, power and energy. [6]
2. Describe how insulation, earthing, fuses and circuit breakers protect users. [4]
3. State the difference between AC and DC. [2]

Section B: Circuits and Current

4. Explain why series or parallel circuits are used for different applications (e.g. household lighting). [3]
5. A resistor has 12 V across it and current of 3 A. Calculate its resistance. [2]
6. Describe how current and voltage behave in series and parallel circuits. [4]

Section C: Charge and Current

7. Explain what electric current is in metals and calculate the charge for 2 A flowing for 3 s. [3]
8. State why lamps and LEDs can indicate the presence of current. [2]

Section D: I–V Characteristics and Sensors

9. Describe the I–V characteristics of a resistor, filament lamp and diode. [4]
10. Explain how resistance changes in an LDR and in a thermistor. [2]

Section E: Power and Energy

11. A device uses 4 A at 12 V. Calculate the power and energy used in 3 minutes. [4]
12. A charge of 0.5 C passes through a component with 9 V across it. Calculate the energy transferred. [2]

Mark Scheme

1. A, C, V, Ω, W, J [6]
2. Insulation prevents contact; earthing provides low-resistance path; fuse melts; circuit breaker switches off [4]
3. AC changes direction; DC flows one direction [2]
4. Series: same current, one failure breaks circuit; parallel: same voltage, components work independently [3]
5. R = V/I = 12/3 = 4 Ω [2]
6. Series: current same, voltage shared; Parallel: voltage same, current splits; current conserved at junction [4]
7. Current is flow of electrons; Q = It = 2 × 3 = 6 C [3]
8. Lamps/LEDs emit light when current flows so show circuit is complete [2]
9. Resistor: straight line; filament lamp: curve; diode: conducts one direction only [4]
10. LDR resistance decreases with light; thermistor resistance decreases with temperature (NTC) [2]
11. P = IV = 48 W; E = IVt = 48 × 180 = 8640 J [4]
12. E = QV = 0.5 × 9 = 4.5 J [2]
